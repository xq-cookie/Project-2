Analysis between area of depriviation and gini coefficient

This project aims to explore the relationship between socioeconomic deprivation and income inequality across the United States. Using datasets on the Area Deprivation Index (ADI) and the Gini Index, we analyze their distributions and correlations to uncover patterns and insights. The analysis is structured as follows:

  1. Analysis of Area Deprivation Index Percent datasets: Examining the distribution of ADI values across states.
  2. Analysis of GINI Data: Investigating income inequality using the Gini Index.
  3. Analysis of merged_data: Combining ADI and Gini datasets to study their relationship.

The findings are visualized through ridge plots and scatter plots, and the merged dataset is saved for further exploration.

Analysis of Area Deprivation Index Percent datasets

In this section, we will analyze the Area Deprivation Index Percent (ADI%) dataset to understand the distribution of socioeconomic deprivation across states. We will visualize the data using ridge plots to highlight disparities and patterns in deprivation levels.

import statsmodels.api as sm

slopes = []

for state, group in merged_data.groupby("state_name"):
    X = sm.add_constant(group["gini_index"])
    y = group["adi_percent"]
    
    try:
        model = sm.OLS(y, X).fit()
        slopes.append({"state": state, "slope": model.params["gini_index"]})
    except:
        pass

slopes_df = pd.DataFrame(slopes).sort_values("slope")

plt.figure(figsize=(10, 14))
sns.barplot(
    data=slopes_df,
    y="state",
    x="slope",
    palette="viridis"
)
plt.title("State-Level Regression Slopes: ADI ~ Gini")
plt.xlabel("Slope (Inequality  Deprivation)")
plt.ylabel("State")
plt.savefig('state_level_regression_slopes.png')
plt.show()

The state-level regression slopes reveal that the relationship between income inequality (Gini Index) and socioeconomic deprivation (ADI Percent) varies substantially across the United States. States such as Massachusetts, Washington, Maryland, New Jersey, and New York exhibit the strongest positive slopes, meaning that higher statewide inequality is strongly associated with higher levels of local deprivation within those states. These states tend to be economically diverse, with sharp contrasts between affluent and disadvantaged communities, which likely amplifies the observed linkage. The presence of several Northeastern and Mid-Atlantic states near the top of the distribution underscores how historical urbanrural divides, dense metropolitan areas, and concentrated high-income sectors contribute to stronger inequalitydeprivation alignment.

Conversely, a number of statesincluding Oklahoma, Utah, Alabama, Oregon, and Floridashow negative or near-zero slopes, indicating little to no measurable relationship between inequality and deprivation levels within their counties. In some cases, negative slopes suggest that counties with higher deprivation may be located in states with relatively lower inequality overall, or that inequality is distributed in ways that do not map cleanly onto local deprivation patterns (e.g., uniformly modest incomes, rural structures, or less variation across counties). The clustering of several Southern and Western states in the negative-slope region implies that in these states, inequality and deprivation do not operate along the same spatial or socioeconomic dimensions. Taken together, the visualization highlights that the relationship between inequality and deprivation is highly state-specific, shaped by each states economic structure, population distribution, and historical development patterns.

Conclusion

The analysis reveals significant insights into the relationship between socioeconomic deprivation and income inequality across the United States:

  1. Area Deprivation Index Percent (ADI%):

    • States exhibit substantial geographic disparities in deprivation levels. Southern and Midwestern states tend to have broader, right-skewed distributions, indicating higher levels of deprivation, while Northeastern and Western states generally show tighter distributions clustered at lower ADI% values.
    • Structural disadvantage is particularly pronounced in states like Mississippi, Louisiana, and West Virginia, whereas states like Utah and Minnesota demonstrate more uniformly advantaged areas.

  2. Gini Index:

    • Income inequality varies across states, with many clustering around moderate levels of inequality. However, states like New York, California, and Texas show wider distributions, reflecting more pronounced disparities.
    • States with narrower distributions, such as Utah and Idaho, suggest more equal income structures.

  3. Merged Analysis (ADI vs Gini):

    • The relationship between inequality and deprivation is highly state-specific. States like Massachusetts and New York show strong positive correlations, indicating that higher inequality is associated with higher deprivation.
    • Conversely, states like Oklahoma and Utah exhibit weak or negative correlations, suggesting that inequality and deprivation may operate independently in these regions.

Overall, the findings highlight that socioeconomic deprivation and income inequality are deeply intertwined but manifest differently across states. Regional patterns are shaped by historical, economic, and demographic factors, underscoring the need for targeted policy interventions to address these disparities. States with strong inequalitydeprivation linkages may benefit from strategies that reduce income disparities, while states with weaker correlations may require localized approaches to tackle deprivation directly.
